## VERSION A: PRE-LAUNCH (Day -3 Teaser)
### Shorter, creates anticipation, ends on a cliff

---

This handbook started as a Confluence page.

I got frustrated watching a new analyst spend three weeks figuring out something I'd worked out 18 months earlier. Not a hard thing. Not classified. Just operational knowledge about how data moves through a federal platform — knowledge that existed in my head and a few other people's heads and nowhere else.

So I wrote it down. For them.

Then someone else on the program asked me a different question. I wrote that down too.

Then I started seeing patterns in what people didn't know — and it wasn't random. The same gaps showed up with every new analyst who joined a federal data program. The same friction. The same wrong assumptions. The same wasted weeks.

A Confluence page turned into a document. The document turned into chapters. The chapters turned into something I realized was too useful to keep inside one program.

Five platforms. Thirteen chapters. 96,000 words.

Coming Thursday.

## VERSION B: POST-LAUNCH (Week 2 Extended Story)
### Full behind-the-scenes with the uncomfortable details

---

Here's how the Federal Data Science Learning Handbook actually got built.

It wasn't a plan. It was accumulated frustration.

I've worked in federal data environments for years — including Palantir AIP at IL5 for the Navy. In that time I watched a pattern repeat itself so consistently it stopped surprising me:

Smart analyst joins a federal data program. They have a degree, maybe a certification, maybe real commercial experience. They sit down at their first classified workstation and spend the next 6 months figuring out why nothing they learned applies.

Not because they're not good. Because the federal data environment is not documented for the people actually trying to work in it.

Vendor documentation is written for commercial deployments. Training courses assume commercial infrastructure. The classified network, the access controls, the governance constraints, the program-specific configurations — none of that exists in any training material.

So the knowledge lived in people's heads. It transferred through conversations. It got partially documented on Confluence pages that two people could access. It got lost every time someone rotated out.

I started writing things down for the people on my programs. That habit compounded over three years.

The uncomfortable truth about writing this handbook:

I rewrote at least four chapters from scratch. Not because I got better at writing — because I kept discovering I'd documented what I thought was true rather than what was actually true. The version of a thing that fits neatly into a chapter is often a simplification that doesn't survive contact with a real program.

Some of the best material in the handbook came from being wrong the first time.

The other thing I didn't expect: how much I learned by trying to explain things clearly. Writing forces rigor in a way that doing doesn't. I thought I understood several of these platforms. Writing about them revealed the edges of my understanding in ways that took months to resolve.

Thirteen chapters. Five platforms. 96,000 words.

One thing I'm certain of: this is the handbook that should have existed before I needed it.

## VERSION C: REFLECTION POST (Month 2 — "What I'd Do Differently")
### For sustained campaign — shows growth and generates commentary

---

Three things I'd do differently if I were writing the Federal Data Science Learning Handbook today.

I published it four weeks ago. Here's what I've already learned from readers that I would incorporate:

**1. More on the human side of federal data work**

The most common feedback I've gotten is that people want more on the organizational dynamics — how to work with data custodians, how to navigate approval chains, how to convince a program manager that the analysis is actually finished. The technical content is solid. The political and interpersonal content is thin.

That's Volume 2 material. Or maybe it's the chapter I should have written better the first time.

**2. The Navy Jupiter chapter needed more external input**

I wrote that chapter mostly from my own experience and a few conversations with colleagues. It's accurate but narrow. I've already had three people send me operational details I didn't have. The chapter will be updated.

**3. The introduction oversells the handbook slightly**

I wrote an introduction that's a little aspirational — "this closes the gap" language that I believe but that should have been qualified. The handbook closes part of the gap for some of the platforms some of the time. The full gap is bigger than 96,000 words.

That's not a failure. That's what Version 1 of anything is. But I should have been more precise about scope.

What it gets right, I'm still proud of: the platform-specific chapters are genuinely useful in a way I've never seen documented elsewhere.

The full handbook is at the link in comments. Feedback and criticism both welcome.
