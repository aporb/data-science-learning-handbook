## "The DoD Has No Standard Data Science Handbook" — Provocative Post

## PRIMARY VERSION: PRE-LAUNCH (Day -10)
### The provocation that primes the audience for the solution

---

The Department of Defense spends billions of dollars on data platforms.

It has enterprise agreements with Palantir, Databricks, Qlik, and others. It built Advana — a DoD-wide data environment that aggregates from hundreds of feeder systems. It stood up the CDAO to drive data-driven decision-making across the entire department.

What it does not have:

A standard data science handbook for the analysts who work on these platforms every day.

Not one. Not a draft. Not a "we're working on it." Nothing.

The onboarding experience for a junior analyst joining a federal data program is, depending on your program: a vendor tutorial written for commercial contexts, a Confluence page maintained by whoever had time, or a conversation with whoever's been there longest.

The knowledge transfer model for federal data science is essentially oral tradition.

We should find that embarrassing.

Not because there aren't talented people doing this work. There are. But because we keep reinventing the same wheel — the same documentation gaps, the same wasted onboarding months, the same re-learning of things that have already been learned and forgotten on a hundred programs before this one.

This is fixable. It doesn't require a program. It doesn't require a budget line.

It requires someone to write it down.

More on that Thursday.

## SECONDARY VERSION: POST-LAUNCH (Week 3-4)
### Now that the handbook exists — the challenge continues

---

Let me make a specific claim and see if anyone pushes back:

The single biggest capability multiplier for federal data programs in the next three years won't be a new platform. It won't be a new AI capability. It won't be a larger budget.

It will be documentation.

Not glamorous. Not fundable. Not the thing anyone puts in a proposal.

But consider: the average federal data analyst spends their first 6 months learning the environment, not doing analysis. Multiply that across the hundreds of new analysts entering federal data programs every year. Multiply it across every platform, every classified enclave, every program that does its onboarding by oral tradition.

That's an extraordinary amount of wasted capability. And most of it is avoidable.

The Federal Data Science Learning Handbook is my attempt to address one piece of it — five platforms, thirteen chapters, 96,000 words, free.

But here's the thing: one handbook isn't the solution. The solution is a culture where documentation is treated as mission-critical work, not overhead.

That's a harder problem. I don't have a 96,000-word handbook for it.

What I do have is an argument: if you run a federal data program and your onboarding relies on someone sitting next to the new analyst for three weeks, you have a documentation gap. That gap is a risk. It will cost you when that person leaves, promotes, or rotates.

Write it down.

Link to the handbook in comments — use it, adapt it, build on it.

What's the one thing on your program that desperately needs to be documented and hasn't been?

## TERTIARY VERSION: THE SHARP TAKE (Month 2)
### For when you want to provoke a real conversation

---

Unpopular opinion in federal data:

We over-invest in platforms and under-invest in the people who use them.

Not just training. Documentation. Knowledge management. The institutional infrastructure that keeps hard-won operational knowledge from walking out the door every time someone PCSes, promotes, or finds a better offer.

The federal government is an extraordinary generator of data science expertise. We have some of the most interesting and consequential data problems in the world — logistics optimization, readiness analytics, intelligence analysis, healthcare at scale.

We are also extraordinarily bad at preserving and transferring the expertise we develop.

The private sector figured this out. Not because tech companies are smarter, but because they measure the cost of knowledge loss. Onboarding time, ramp time, error rates in the first 6 months — these get tracked, analyzed, and optimized.

In federal data programs, the cost of documentation gaps is mostly invisible. It shows up as "the analyst needed more time than expected" on a program schedule. It doesn't show up as a line item.

The Federal Data Science Learning Handbook is an attempt to make some of that invisible cost visible — by just writing the thing down.

What we really need is for this to become normal. Not one person writing one handbook. A culture of documentation at the program level.

That's a longer conversation. I'm interested in having it.

What's your experience with knowledge management on federal data programs?

## POSTING NOTES

- The Day -10 version is the most important — it establishes the problem before you present the solution. The announcement post lands harder when the audience has already been thinking about the gap.
- The post-launch versions are designed to extend the conversation rather than re-announce the handbook
- Expect pushback on the "unpopular opinion" version — that's the point. Engage with pushback substantively and specifically, not defensively
- If any senior DoD officials or CDAO staff engage, reply with a DM in addition to a public comment
- These posts tend to perform well with federal employees and less well with vendors — that's correct. The target audience here is practitioners and program managers, not platform sales teams.
